# Part C: Critical Comparison & Reflection

## 1. Which model performed better and why?

The best-performing model overall was the **Advanced MLP from Part B**. More specifically, the **untuned Advanced MLP** achieved the strongest **test performance**, with a test accuracy and macro F1-score of **0.9972**, while the **tuned Advanced MLP** achieved the strongest **validation performance**, with validation accuracy and macro F1-score of **0.9974**. Both Advanced MLP versions clearly outperformed the **Basic MLP** from Part A, which achieved **0.9867** test accuracy and macro F1, and the **SLP baseline**, which achieved **0.8960** test accuracy and **0.8950** macro F1.

The Advanced MLP performed better because it used a more sophisticated architecture. Compared with the earlier models, it included **multiple hidden layers, Batch Normalization, Dropout, L2 regularisation, EarlyStopping, and ReduceLROnPlateau**. These additions helped the model train more stably, reduced overfitting, and improved its ability to learn more complex patterns in the dataset. Another important strength was that the advanced model did not only perform well in terms of classification accuracy, but also showed strong confidence behaviour. For example, the tuned Advanced MLP flagged **82.76%** of its errors using a low-confidence threshold, showing that it was also effective at identifying uncertain predictions.

## 2. How did your independent thinking compare to GenAI assistance?

My independent thinking was most important in **Part A**, because that is where I built the core project foundation myself. I made the main decisions about data loading, data quality checks, EDA, preprocessing, train/validation/test splitting, scaling, baseline model design, and the overall evaluation structure. This part showed that I could create a complete ANN workflow on my own and understand the problem properly before relying on outside assistance. The strong performance of the Basic MLP in Part A also proved that my own reasoning was effective.

GenAI assistance was more useful in **Part B**, where the goal was to extend the project using a more advanced model. AI helped by suggesting a deeper MLP architecture, stronger regularisation methods, callback-based training control, and a focused tuning strategy. This made the Part B model more sophisticated and helped structure the comparison more clearly. However, I do not think AI replaced my thinking. Instead, it acted as a support tool that helped refine and extend ideas that were already grounded in my own understanding of the dataset and workflow.

## 3. Did AI miss anything important or surprise you?

Yes, AI did miss some important points, mainly because it could only suggest ideas in a general way and could not know the exact behaviour of my dataset in advance. For example, AI could recommend tuning and architecture improvements, but it could not predict that the **untuned Advanced MLP would slightly outperform the tuned version on the test set**. That was something I only discovered through actual experimentation. In the same way, AI could not automatically know which class would be the hardest, which confusion pairs would appear most often, or how well the low-confidence rule would detect errors.

The main surprise was that the **tuned model gave the best validation score but not the best test score**. This was important because it showed that tuning does not always guarantee better generalisation. Another surprising and positive result was how strong the confidence analysis turned out to be. The final tuned Advanced MLP caught **24 out of 29 errors** through low-confidence flagging, which shows that the model was not only accurate but also reasonably good at identifying when it might be wrong. That result added much more depth to the project than just reporting classification accuracy alone.

## 4. How would you balance AI assistance vs critical thinking in future tasks?

In future tasks, I would use AI as a **support tool**, but not as a replacement for my own judgement. AI is very useful for brainstorming ideas, suggesting model improvements, giving coding structure, and proposing practical tuning strategies. It can speed up the process and expose me to techniques I may not think of immediately. However, the most important parts of a technical project still need critical thinking from me. These include understanding the problem, checking data quality, deciding whether an AI suggestion is appropriate, evaluating fairness in comparisons, and interpreting results properly.

This project showed me that the best balance is to let AI help with **possible options**, while I remain responsible for **final decisions**. For example, I accepted suggestions such as Batch Normalization, Dropout, L2 regularisation, and callback-based training because they were suitable for the dataset and improved the model. At the same time, I rejected ideas such as class weighting, oversampling, and focal loss because the dataset was already balanced and those methods were not necessary. So in future work, I would continue using AI to improve efficiency and idea generation, but I would rely on critical thinking to test, question, and validate everything before accepting it. That balance is what makes the final work both stronger and more academically credible.
